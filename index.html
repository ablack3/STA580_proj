The association between insurance status, use of primary care, and diagnosis of chronic illness

Andrew Deighan and Adam Black

4/25/2017

Introduction

Utilization of primary care has been shown time and time again to improve health outcomes and lower costs. Primary care is a critical component of the health system, able to act as the initial access point to the healthcare system as a whole, and to fulfill the majority of people’s needs[1]. Even in cases where specialized care is needed, better health outcomes are regularly achieved when this specialized care is coordinated through a primary care provider [1]. From the county level to the national level, accessibility to primary care is strongly associated with improved general health along with decreased mortality rates, hospital admissions, overall healthcare system costs, and socioeconomic disparities [1].

In the years following the passage of the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (ACA) the uninsured rate has dropped from around 18% in 2013 to around 9% in 2015 [2,3]. This drop can be attributed largely to the expansion of the government funded Medicaid program in many states and the introduction of subsidized individual marketplaces for health insurance introduced by the ACA [2]. With this recent boost in the proportion of Americans with health insurance come important questions about the overall effects of this shift on the healthcare system.

We aim to investigate the relationship between insurance status, utilization of primary care, and subsequent diagnosis of chronic illness in the United States. If insurance status is indeed linked to increased utilization of primary care then the reduction in the uninsured rate could herald future overall improvement of health outcomes and decreases in costs. However if greater use of primary care leads to overdiagnosis of chronic illness the resulting effect on health care costs could be quite the opposite.

Methods

Data

To investigate how presence of insurance is linked with utilization of primary care and the diagnosis of chronic illness we examined data from the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS), 2011-2015. The BRFSS telephone survey is a joint effort between individual state and territory health departments and the CDC and asks respondents a variety of questions about their health behaviors, health status, and demographics [4].

The questions we use from the survey are as follows:

Measure of insurance status

  1. Insurance Status: Do you have any kind of health care coverage, including health insurance, prepaid plans such as HMOs, or government plans such as Medicare, or Indian Health Service?
  • 1: Yes
  • 2: No
  • 7: Don’t know/Not Sure
  • 9: Refused

Measures of primary care access and utilization

  1. Personal Doctor: Do you have one person you think of as your personal doctor or healthcare provider?
  • 1: Yes, only one
  • 2: More than one
  • 3: No
  • 7: Don’t know/Not Sure
  • 9: Refused

The personal doctor question was collapsed into a binary variable (Yes/No).

  1. Doctor Utilization: About how long has it been since you last visited a doctor for a routine checkup?
  • 1: Within past year (anytime less than 12 months ago)
  • 2: Within past 2 years (1 year but less than 2 years ago)
  • 3: Within past 5 years (2 years but less than 5 years ago)
  • 4: 5 or more years ago
  • 7: Don’t know/Not sure
  • 8: Never
  • 9: Refused

We examined the question about utilization by looking at two binary variables derived from it:

  • Had checkup within one year vs. Have not had checkup within one year
  • Have had checkup within past 5 years vs. Have not had a checkup within past 5 years

Measures of chronic illness diagnosis

We examined 10 chronic illnesses: diabetes, heart attack, asthma, arthritis, copd, depression, stroke, kidney disease, skin cancer, and other cancer. The questions about chronic illness were generally worded as

  1. Has a doctor ever told you that you have [insert chronic condition]?
  • 1: Yes
  • 2: No
  • 7: Don’t know/Not Sure
  • 9: Refused

Finally we used a question to compare the general health status of people with a PCP to those without a PCP. The responses were grouped into two levels to create a binary general health variable.

5. Would you say your general health status is

  • 1: Excellent, very good, or good
  • 2: Fair or poor

Demographic variables used in analysis of chronic illness

Age and income are important confounding variables for the relationship between utilization and chronic condition diagnosis. We created two categorical variables to capture the age and income characteristics of the respondents.

6. Age group

  • 40 - 54
  • 55 - 64
  • 65 - 74
  • 75 and older

7. Household income

  • Less than $25,000 per year
  • Between $25,000 and $50,000 per year
  • Greater than $50,000 per year

Any refusals to respond or responses of “Unsure/Don’t Know” for any of the variables we used were ignored. Thus, only full responses on the variables above were included and all analysis variables except for age and income were binary. Because the datasets are very large and the fraction of excluded responses was negligible. See table A17 in the appendix for the numbers of observations eliminated.

Statistical analysis

In order to explore the relationship between insurance status, PCP use, and diagnosis of chronic illness we examine four associations.

  1. Insurance status and having a PCP
  2. Insurance status and having a checkup in the past year
  3. Insurance status and having  a checkup in the past five years
  4. Having a PCP and being diagnosed with one of 10 chronic illnesses

The first three relationships can described by 2x2 tables and were examined using Pearson’s chi squared test of association, rank correlation measures (gamma & tau b), relative risk, and absolute risk difference.

To investigate the association between having a PCP and diagnosis of chronic illness the population was stratified by age and income. The relative risk of diagnosis was calculated for each strata and each conditions. In addition a logistic regression model was fit using a dependent indicator variable for diagnosis of any of the 10 chronic illnesses. Having a personal doctor was the independent variable of interest and age and income were included for adjustment. This model was fit separately for each year and for all years combined.

Results

Personal doctor ~ Insurance

Figure 1: The proportion of insured and uninsured individuals who reported having a personal doctor.

It is evident from figure 1 that the proportion of individuals that have a personal doctor is higher for those with insurance than those without and that this relationship is consistent from 2011 through 2015. The Pearson chi-squared tests of general association were highly significant (all p < 0.0001) confirming the presence of a general association between insurance status and having a personal doctor for every year from 2011 through 2015. For the calculated test-statistics see table A1 in the appendix.

As we would expect, the Kendall’s tau-b and Goodman and Kruskal’s gamma statistics confirm that the direction of the association is towards insured individuals being more likely to have a personal doctor. Table 1, gives these concordance statistics calculated for all years combined. For information on the rank correlation statistics for specific years see table A2 in the appendix. The narrow confidence intervals indicate that the positive correlation between insurance status and having a personal doctor is highly statistically significant.

The difference in the magnitude of the tau-b and gamma statistics is due to the fact that the gamma statistic looks at the difference between the number of concordant and discordant pairs relative to the total number of only discordant or concordant pairs and, as such, ties do not affect the gamma statistic. On the other hand, the tau-b statistic does take ties into account. For more information on the performance of the Pearson chi-squared test and the calculation of the rank correlation statistics please see the appendix.

Figure 2: Point estimates and 95% CI for relative and absolute differences in PCP access by insurance

Figure 2 shows that the absolute and relative differences in PCP access between insured and uninsured individuals are both statistically and practically significant across time. For specific values see tables A7 and A8 in the appendix. There appears to be a downward trend in the absolute difference from 2011 to 2015 but since the range of the y-axis is small the magnitude of the reduction is actually quite small. Moreover, it is clear from the error bars that the confidence intervals for each consecutive year overlap with each other and even the confidence intervals for 2011 and 2015 are close. We can conclude that there is a strong and consistent association between insurance status and PCP access.

The small confidence intervals indicate that the positive correlation between insurance status and doctor utilization is highly statistically significant. The green dots and corresponding confidence intervals nearer the top of the plots are the observed differences when considering all respondents to the BRFSS. The red dots are the observed differences when we restricted our analysis to only individuals that have a personal doctor.

The absolute and relative differences shown in figure 4 make it clear that insured individuals are much more likely to have had a general health checkup recently, even when considering only people who have a personal doctor. When looking at all years combined insured individuals were about 1.84 times more likely (relative difference ~1.84) to have had a general health checkup within the past year than uninsured individuals were. This difference was noticeably more muted when only considering individuals that had a personal doctor, but even then the difference was large. Insured individuals with a personal doctor were about 1.37 times as likely to have had checkup within a year than uninsured individuals. For specific values see tables A9 through A12.

Figure 5: Absolute and relative differences between the proportion of uninsured individuals that have not had a checkup within 5 years and the proportion of insured who have not. The green dots and corresponding confidence intervals nearer the top of the plots are the observed differences when considering all respondents to the BRFSS. The red dots are the observed differences when we restricted our analysis to only individuals that have a personal doctor.

Figure 5 shows the absolute and relative differences between the proportion of uninsured individuals that have not had a checkup within five years and the proportion of insured individuals that have not. Note that in this case the difference is between uninsured and insured, not the other way around. The absolute difference of 0.182 for all individuals means the proportion of insured individuals that have not had a checkup within five years is 0.182 less than the proportion of uninsured individuals that have not. For information on specific figures see tables A13 - A16 in the appendix. The absolute differences were much less when considering only individuals with personal doctors than when considering all individuals. However, when we look at the relative difference we see that for both groups (all individuals and only those that have a personal doctor), uninsured individuals are much more likely to not have had a checkup within 5 years than uninsured individuals.

The degree of the relationship between insurance status and frequency of general health checkups decreased slightly from 2011 to 2015. The relative difference between the proportion of insured individuals who have had a checkup within one year and the proportion of uninsured individuals who have decreased from ~1.90 (95% CI: 1.8806,1.9126) in 2011 to ~1.75 (95% CI: 1.7357, 1.7715) in 2015. However, the relationship between insurance status and the proportion of individuals who have not had checkup within five years did not change significantly from 2011 to 2015.

In summary, individuals with health insurance, when looking at all years combined, are estimated to be 2.12 times as likely to have a personal doctor and 1.84 times as likely to have had a general health checkup within a year. On the other hand, individuals without health insurance are estimated to be 3.87 times as likely to not have had checkup within five years. The differences in checkup frequency are less drastic when considering only individuals with a personal doctor, but they are still large. Among individuals who reported having a doctor, insured individuals are estimated to be 1.38 times as likely to have had a checkup within a year and 3.29 times less likely to have not had a checkup within five years. The 95% confidence intervals for all these estimates are quite narrow, indicating that the estimated relative differences in proportion are likely close to the actual relative differences in the population.

Chronic illness DX ~ PCP

We investigated the association between having a primary care doctor and being diagnosed with one of 10 chronic illnesses: arthritis, asthma, COPD, depression, diabetes, heart attack, kidney disease, stoke, skin cancer, and cancer other than skin cancer. The questions were generally worded “Have you ever been diagnosed with _____?” implying that we are measuring prevalence and not incidence.

Since age and income are associated with both having a PCP and development of chronic illness we stratified our analysis by these variables. Age was broken into four categories: 40-54, 55-65, 65-75, >75. Annual Household income was broken into three categories: <25K, 25-50K, >50K. The ratio of the risk of diagnosis in those with a PCP to the risk of diagnosis in those without a PCP was calculated for each combination of age and income group. The results are shown in figure/table X. These were calculated by combining all years 2011-2015 together in order increase sample size. The results did not appear to differ in a meaningful way from year to year.

The relative risk of diagnosis of chronic illness is shown below in figure X.

Figure 6: Relative risk of diagnosis (has PCP/no PCP) stratified by age and income. 2011-2015 combined.

Nearly all of the relative risk values are significantly greater than one and most are greater than 1.5. People with a personal doctor (PCP) are much more likely than those without a personal doctor to receive a diagnosis for a chronic illness or cancer. Based on the general health variable it does appear that people without a personal doctor are somewhat healthier than those with a doctor. However it does not seem likely that this small difference accounts for the magnitude and consistency of the differences in diagnosis rates across conditions, years, age and income categories. When we examined the risk of being in fair or poor general health between those with a PCP and those without, the difference was small. See appendix for these risk ratios and their confidence intervals. This indicates that people without a PCP are not so much healthier than those without as to account for the striking difference in diagnosis rates.

For some chronic illnesses the difference in diagnosis rates seems to be more affected by age and income than others. For example the young (40-55) and poor (<25K) without a PCP are over 2.5 times more likely to be diagnosed with skin cancer than their counterparts who have a PCP.

In most chronic illnesses we see the difference between those with a PCP and those without getting smaller as people age. Insurance status certainly changes as people hit age 65 and qualify for Medicare. As a result the number of people without a PCP drops as age increases particularly at the age 65 cutoff. We might hypothesize that one reason the differences disappear as people get older is that those without a PCP at an old age have probably had a PCP at some point in the past and may have had the opportunity to have their health issues diagnosed. It may also be that a survivor effect is taking place. The people who, for example, had undiagnosed cancer when they are age 50 may not be alive at age 75, thus causing the sample of people without a PCP to become biased toward healthier people who have survived without medical care as age increases. These are speculations and would require follow up analysis for further investigation

The outcome was any diagnosis of a chronic condition (Yes/No) and the explanatory variables were age, income, and access to a PCP. Clearly there is a strong association between having a personal doctor and being diagnosed with a chronic illness.

Discussion

Our measures of primary care access and utilization were all strongly associated with insurance. The relationship between insurance status and primary care utilization was less strong when considering only individuals that have a personal doctor. However, for all three measures having insurance was positively correlated with primary care access and utilization.

Our analysis indicates that insurance status in the United States is indeed correlated with better primary care access and utilization. The presence of this relationship is promising because it would imply that as uninsured rates drop in the United States, access to primary care should be expected to rise, and the subsequent improvements in health outcomes and reduction in costs should hopefully follow.

In addition to showing the association between insurance status and having a PCP we have also investigated the association between having a PCP and likelihood of diagnosis of a chronic illness or cancer. The extent to which the likelihood of diagnosis differs between those with a PCP and those without across age, income, and condition might indicate widespread underdiagnosis of these chronic illnesses and cancer in the uninsured population. If this is the case then expansion of access to primary care through expanded insurance coverage should substantially improve population health by leading to earlier diagnoses of chronic health problems and cancer.

On the other hand it is possible that the difference in diagnoses rates is at least partly due to overdiagnosis of chronic illness in the insured population. What constitutes an unnecessary diagnosis is a matter of debate. The truth is probably somewhere in the middle and knowing how much of these diagnoses represent overdiagnosis is critical to being able to understanding the effects of the increase in the insured population on overall health care costs and outcomes in the US.
